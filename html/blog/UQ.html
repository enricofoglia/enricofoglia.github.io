Uncertainty quantification: teach neural nets to doubt themselves

Published on November 25, 2024 | 5 min read

In my research, I deal with a particular mix of aeroacoustics, which is the science of predicting sound produced by unsteady aerodynamics, and deep learning. Crucially, aeroacoustics is extremely delicate, and either needs very high-fidelity simulations or precise experiments to be investigated. This means, that any dataset about aeroacoustics is, by default, going to be quite limited in terms of datapoints. The question is:

How can one plan new experiments (numerical or physical) by taking into account what the model does not know yet?

In order to do so, it is important to estimate the uncertainties related in the model and the data. In general, one can identify two types of uncertainty: the one that comes from data and the one that comes from the model itself.

Aleatoric uncertainty: noise in the data

It is rare, in real-world situations, to have perfectly clean data. In particular, some amount of noise or small measurement errors are always going to be present, especially for experimental data. This kind of uncertainty is the same one involved in games of luck, like dice or cards: even if the rules are perfectly clear, the outcome is always unpredictable because of the random nature of the process. For example, everybody knows that the probability distribution of the numbers of a die are all equal to 1/6, and no matter how many games you play, the probability will always stay at 1/6. Because of this analogy, the uncertainty that is inherent in the data-generating process is called aleatoric, from the Latin word for gambler. Fundamentally, this kind of uncertainty is irreducible: you won't be able to reduce the noise in your sensors by gathering more data, at best you will predict it better. This type of uncertainty can be accurately predicted given a large enough dataset of high-quality measurements. In fact, any classifier is already trying its best at predicting a probability distribution over the labels, rather than a single answer, by passing the last layer through the famous softmax activation. The fact that most classifiers seem overconfident, is that they are usually trained to give a probability close to 1 to their top answer! How can we know if a model is truly confident in its answer, or if it is only guessing a high probability?

Epistemic uncertainty: modeling mistakes

The second type of uncertainty comes from the way the data is modelled. No model is perfect, and no model is ever going to be able to generalize flawlessly to data it has never seen before: what is important for trust-worthy AI is a model that can recognize its own limits. The key to unlock such ability is a correct estimation of epistemic uncertainty, from the Greek word έπιστήμη, which roughly translates to knowledge or science. It is particularly difficult to model this type of uncertainty because you are not asking the network to predict an external process, but to predict something about its own internal degree of belief. Going back to the die example, epistemic uncertainty would quantify how much you expect the game to be rigged: after some games, you might get the feeling that something is off, that some numbers are appearing more than they should. This uncertainty about your model of the world (id est that all faces are equally probable) is the epistemic, or model uncertainty. Importantly, this kind of uncertainty can be reduced by gathering more data: after observing many games with the weighted die, you can reconstruct the true probabilities by computing some statistics! Because of this, epistemic uncertainty is said to be reducible. Thus, epistemic uncertainty is the metric that one has to use to identify areas of the data space where it would be more interesting to gather more evidence to improve the model. One of the key challenges in uncertainty quantification is to separate the aleatoric and epistemic parts: if the model gives probability 50% to an event, does it mean that it is sure that the real phenomenon is stochastic with probability 1/2, or is it just unsure of the correct anwer?

Some math

We start from the data. Let the input $X$ and the output $Y$ be joint random variables with distribution $p(X,Y)$. Once we have chosen a particular input $x$, the remaining uncertainty on $Y$ is given by the conditional distribution $p(Y\vert x)$. This uncertainty is inherent to the data, and thus it is the aleatoric part of the uncertainty. As we have seen before, the best we can do about this type of uncertainty is to accurately predict it, so we define a model $p_\theta(Y\vert x)$ to approximate the true distribution of the data. The difference between $p$ and $p_\theta$ is the epistemic part of the uncertainty, because is due to imperfections in the model.

To quantify this difference we need a way to compare probability distributions. This is done with the help of scoring rules: a scoring rule is a function $\phi$ that takes as input a probability distribution $p$ and a data point $Y$ and outputs a real number. To compare two distributions $p$ and $q$ we can use the expected score, defined as: $$ s_\phi(p,q) = \mathbb{E}_{Y\sim q}[\phi(p,Y)]\;. $$ The expected score does not need to be zero if the two distributions are equal. To get rid of this annoyance, one can simply subtract $s_\phi(q,q)$ from the previous expression, to get the divergence: $$ d_{\phi}(p,q) = s_\phi(p,q)-s_{\phi}(q,q) $$ Now, if $\phi$ is a proper scoring rule, $d_\phi(p,q)$ will be zero if $p$ and $q$ are equal. When optimizing a neural net we just try to minimize $\mathbb{E}[\phi(p_\theta,Y)]$, the expectation of the scoring rule between the model and the true data distribution. This loss can be decomposed as follows: $$ \mathbb{E}[\phi(p_\theta,Y)] = \mathbb{E}[d_\phi(p_\theta,p)] + \mathbb{E}[\phi(p,Y)] $$
